Contemporary Scleral Lenses

Interactive reading map for Ophthalmology: Current and Future Developments, Volume 4 - Contemporary Scleral Lenses: Theory and Application, edited by Melissa Barnett and Lynette K. Johns.

Fast Summary

This volume is a practical guide to scleral lenses: why they returned to clinical use, how their anatomy and optics work, who benefits, how to fit and evaluate them, how to document care, and how to manage complications and special cases.

Core Thesis

Scleral lenses succeed because they vault the cornea, land on the conjunctival/scleral surface, and create a fluid reservoir that can both rehabilitate optics and protect compromised ocular surfaces. Their power comes with clinical responsibility: oxygen delivery, reservoir thickness, landing-zone alignment, handling, hygiene, and follow-up all matter.

Most Important Points

  • History: scleral lenses began as glass shells for protection and optics, declined with smaller corneal lenses, then re-emerged because modern GP materials, imaging, and manufacturing solved earlier problems.
  • Terminology: lens size should be described by bearing pattern: corneal, corneo-scleral, or scleral. Scleral designs include optic, mid-peripheral/intermediate, and landing zones.
  • Shape drives fit: corneal curvature alone is not enough. Ocular sagittal height, limbal contour, nasal flattening, toricity, and quadrant asymmetry determine vault and landing.
  • Optics: the post-lens tear reservoir masks irregularity, but decentration, tilt, reservoir depth, front-surface optics, and higher-order aberrations affect final vision.
  • Indications: major uses include irregular corneas, ectasia, post-surgical corneas, grafts, ocular surface disease, exposure, neurotrophic disease, persistent epithelial defects, trauma, and some regular-cornea refractive needs.
  • Ideal fit: clear the cornea and limbus without excessive reservoir thickness; align the landing zone to avoid compression, impingement, blanching, edge lift, or suction.
  • Evaluation: inspect for bubbles and wetting first, then measure apical and limbal clearance, judge landing-zone patterns, assess movement/decentration, and re-check after settling.
  • Complications: watch for edema, bullae, neovascularization, limbal bearing, staining, hyperemia, conjunctival impression, hypertrophy, prolapse, midday fogging, poor wetting, poor vision, and handling injury.
  • Handling: patient success depends on clean hands, correct filling solution, bubble-free application, safe removal, disinfection, case hygiene, and repeated education.
  • Practice systems: good outcomes depend on documentation, imaging, staff training, referral communication, realistic expectations, and collaborative care with ophthalmology.

Clinical Mental Model

Think in five layers: patient indication, ocular shape, lens geometry, physiologic response, and real-world handling. A lens that looks optically impressive but causes compression, hypoxia, fogging, or handling failure is not a successful lens.
